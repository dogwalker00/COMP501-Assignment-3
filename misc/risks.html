Risks for Robotics in Medicine and Healthcare

Despite the numerous opportunities that robotics brings into medicine and healthcare, there are also several risks and challenges. In July of 2019, the Ministry of Health of New Zealand released advice for the Emerging Health Technology for robotics in healthcare and medicine focusing on the New Zealand public health.

One of the primary challenges mentioned was the adoption of modern technologies due to financial constraints. Many pioneering technologies remain out of reach for individuals as they are not publicly funded, further exacerbating the issue of inequality and division between the privileged and marginalized communities.

The integration of innovative technologies imposes the challenges of retraining healthcare and medical professionals. The government must invest in comprehensive training programs to ensure the workforce is equipped with essential competences to leverage the benefits of recent technologies. Failure to do so may see a mismatch between new technological capabilities and current skillsets, forecasting a potential gap in the workforce.

Furthermore, the government must also consider the upfront investment cost that are needed for a successful implementation, such as maintenance, upgrades, and security patching. Comprehensive strategies must be established to ensure long-standing sustainability and effectiveness, as well as continuous improvements and adaptations in technologies.

Another challenge that serves as a concern for the public is the perception of robots stealing jobs. While technological growth may automate certain tasks, it is critical to highlight that the role of medical and healthcare professionals cannot be entirely replaced. Rather, the advancement should be perceived as a tool that enhances the capabilities of these technologies, enabling attention on more complex and personalized care.

Moreover, the aversion and skepticism towards innovative technologies also obstruct its extensive adoption, as people require satisfactory knowledge and information to feel comfortable in accepting this progression. Without comprehensive understanding and transparency, there may be hesitation within the community to integrate these advancing technologies.

Additionally, the nervousness surrounding the impersonal nature of technology-powered encounters may make patients apprehensive. Therefore, it is essential to not sacrifice compassionate and empathetic interaction due to technological integration between patients and medical and healthcare professionals.
